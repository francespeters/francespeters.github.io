MOHAI MAP REDESIGN

June 2024 - August 2024

UX Design | Prototyping | Usability Studies

Disciplines: UX Design | User Flow Mapping | User Research | Prototyping | Wire Frames

Tools: Figma | Adobe Photoshop

Project Overview

This project was the final capstone for my User Experience and Design Program in Seattle, WA. The program provided a foundation in Human-Computer Interaction and industry-standard design practices while connecting students with real-world sponsors seeking design improvements. Over six weeks, I led my team in an evidence-based, user-centered design process, resulting in a functional prototype that represented our sponsor’s needs.

Competitor Research

The design process started with investigating exisiting implementations of interactive museum maps and navigation tools. We analyzed both digital and physical solutions, identifying common pain points such as information overload, lack of interactivity, and poor accessibility. This research informed our design decisions, ensuring that our solution would address real user needs while standing out in the museum space.

1. Legit Project: Legit Budapest Visitor Center

Background: The Legit Budapest Visitor center acts as a starting point for visitors in Budapest. It features a highly detailed, 3D-printed model of Budapest combined with numerous handheld tablets that allow users to view different information regarding landmarks in the city by selecting different pins.

  • Strengths

  • - Centerpoint: The model itself is a tourist destination drawing people in to learn more about the city due to its detail and size.

  • - Interactive: The tablet allows users to interact with the physical model using an online interface

  • - Personalization: Allows for personalization of the existing map regarding what a user wants to learn more about.

  • - Free of Charge: The users do not have to pay to view the map or use the tablets.

  • Weaknesses

  • - Less Accessibility: Requires the user to possess a higher skill set when navigating the tablet interface, for example, the tablet must calibrate in order to properly represent and translate the 3D-model.

  • - Implementation requires a high budget: The model itself was entirely 3D-printed requiring many resources making this design challenging to recreate

User Research

The design process started with interviewing 5 museum goers to better understand the current limitations of the museum’s static map design and identify opportunities for improvement.

  • Key Quotes

  • “I want to choose the level of engagement rather than it being presented to me.”
  • “It’s pretty memorable for a lot of people too just because it's highly interactive.”
  • The interactive and relatable elements of existing maps “gives visitors maybe like ‘oh ok maybe I should go visit that neighborhood and see whats it’s all about’.”
  • “If there's not a key and there's symbols on there, you’re going to need to interpret those for me. I’m not going to pick up on them.”
  • "I think [the interactive map] should be more prominent. When I walk in as a visitor, to me it's just a gray/black box. You don’t really notice it until you walk up to it.”

Ideation & Lo-Fidelity Prototyping

Our team brainstormed solutions utilizing common design practices such as storyboarding, user flow mapping, and quick wireframes. We started to refine our design with low-fidelity prototypes.

High-Fidelity Prototyping

Our high-fidelity prototype was designed using Figma. To integrate preexisting maps from the museum Adobe Photoshop was used to edit and adapt the maps while preserving key information.

Usability Testing

We conducted four usability tests to evaluate our design, identify weaknesses, and establish goals for future iterations.
